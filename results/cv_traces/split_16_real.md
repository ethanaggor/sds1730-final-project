## Analysis Framework

I'll work through this systematically by identifying the key relationships in the training data, then applying them to each held-out state.

### Key Patterns in Training Data

**1. Appreciation is the dominant predictor, but not sufficient alone.**

The "Sand States" (NV, AZ, FL) show the clearest pattern: extreme appreciation (73-80%) combined with high population growth (7-12%) produced catastrophic crashes (-44% to -56%). But DC had 70% appreciation and only crashed -12%, because government employment and high income created a floor.

**2. Population growth amplifies bubbles into crashes.**

High pop growth drives speculative construction. When demand collapses, oversupply hammers prices. Key signal: NV (12.18% pop growth, -55.69% crash) vs MD (2.39% pop growth, -25.30% crash), despite similar appreciation levels.

**3. Supply constraints massively dampen crashes.**

DC (-0.17 crash/appreciation ratio) and VT (-0.16 ratio) show that markets where you can't easily build new housing resist crashes, even with high appreciation. Compare to unconstrained sand states (-0.60 to -0.70 ratio).

**4. Manufacturing decline creates crashes independent of bubbles.**

MI: 8.86% appreciation but -29.40% crash. OH: 9.43% appreciation but -12.93%. The auto industry collapse drove housing declines that had nothing to do with bubble dynamics.

**5. Energy/agricultural economies resist crashes.**

ND (0.00%), SD (0.00%), IA (0.00%) all had modest appreciation and stable demand-side fundamentals from commodity sectors.

**6. The New England cluster crashes consistently.**

CT (-18.76%), NH (-19.98%), MA (-17.56%) all crashed -17 to -20% despite varying appreciation (28-37%). RI was worse (-27.60%) at higher appreciation (48.54%). VT is the outlier (-6.55%) due to extreme rural supply constraints.

### TX (Texas): **-5.50%**

**Comparables:** NE (11.88% appr → -2.88%), KS (12.49% → -4.97%)

Texas is the most defensible mild-crash prediction. With only 11.68% appreciation, Texas barely experienced a bubble. The state's unique home equity lending restrictions (80% LTV cap, refinance limitations) prevented the kind of overleveraging that devastated other states. Texas also has extremely elastic housing supply; builders could keep up with the 6.03% population growth without price distortion. The energy sector (oil prices remained strong through mid-2008) provided employment stability. Despite high pop growth, the regulatory framework and supply elasticity kept the crash mild. Slightly worse than NE/KS due to higher pop growth and UR.
