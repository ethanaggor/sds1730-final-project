## Analysis Framework

I'll work through the training data to identify the key drivers, then apply those patterns to each held-out state.

### Key Patterns in Training Data

**1. Appreciation is the primary crash predictor**, but the relationship is modulated by other factors:
- Appreciation < 15%: crashes typically -3% to -8% (exceptions: MI at -29.4% from auto collapse, GA at -24.4% from Atlanta subprime)
- Appreciation 15-25%: crashes typically -8% to -14% (exceptions: MN/IL ~-22% from large metro exposure, ND/SD at 0% from rural stability)
- Appreciation 25-45%: wide range -7% to -27%, depending on speculation and income
- Appreciation > 50%: typically -25% to -56%

**2. Population growth amplifies the crash** by proxying speculative/supply-side overbuilding: NV (12.18% pop, -55.69%), AZ (9.41%, -46.01%), FL (6.84%, -44.73%), ID (7.72%, -26.95%).

**3. High income buffers the crash**: DC (70.23% appreciation, $55K income, only -11.93%), HI (80.67% appreciation, only -18.48%), CT ($53K income, -18.76% despite 36.58% appreciation).

**4. High pre-crash unemployment signals structural economic weakness** that worsens the downturn independently of bubble size: MI (only 8.86% appreciation, 6.87% UR, -29.40%).

### LA (Louisiana)
- **Appreciation**: 22.89% (moderate)
- **UR**: 4.14%, **PCPI**: $33,627, **Pop growth**: -4.83% (extreme negative, Katrina)

**Comparables by appreciation**: MN (24.94%, -21.83%), AR (20.02%, -6.70%), ND (22.30%, 0.00%). **No real comparable for the -4.83% pop loss.**

Louisiana's -4.83% population growth is the extreme outlier, driven entirely by Hurricane Katrina (August 2005). This makes standard comparisons unreliable. Katrina simultaneously destroyed housing supply and displaced demand. The net price effect was complex: destroyed housing reduced supply (supporting prices), federal aid and insurance payouts injected capital, and rebuilding activity stimulated construction. Baton Rouge actually saw price increases as displaced New Orleans residents relocated. The moderate pre-storm appreciation (22.89%) indicates a limited bubble. The combination of supply destruction, federal capital injection, and absence of speculative overbuilding meant Louisiana's housing correction was driven by the national recession, not a local bubble popping.

**Prediction: -6.0%** (moderate national-recession drag, heavily buffered by Katrina supply dynamics)
