## Analysis Framework

From the training data, I identify these key patterns:

1. **Appreciation is the primary crash predictor**, but the relationship is non-linear and modulated by other factors.
2. **Speculative dynamics amplify crashes**: high appreciation + high population growth = worst outcomes (NV, AZ, FL).
3. **Supply constraints buffer crashes**: HI (80.67% appreciation, only -18.48%) and DC (70.23%, only -11.93%) show that constrained supply and stable demand bases moderate busts.
4. **Structural employment shocks can drive crashes without bubbles**: GA (-24.38% with only 15.17% appreciation) shows that overbuilding and job losses can devastate markets independent of bubble size.
5. **Stable/diversified economies with low speculation are resilient**: IA, ND, SD at 0%; TX, OK, NE all under -5%.

## State-by-State Predictions

### KY (Kentucky) — predicted: **-7.50**

Appreciation of 14.25% is low, comparable to OK (14.28% -> -3.57), KS (12.49% -> -4.97), and IN (8.68% -> -7.30). However, KY's elevated UR (5.73%) and low PCPI ($30,476) signal structural economic weakness more akin to TN (17.18%, 5.22% UR -> -8.92) and MS (16.40%, 6.38% UR -> -8.67). Kentucky's economy mixes coal, tobacco, manufacturing, and services with no major speculative housing market. Louisville and Lexington had some subprime exposure, but nothing extreme. Slightly less appreciation than TN/MS but worse UR than IN.

### OH (Ohio) — predicted: **-10.00**

Very low appreciation (9.43%) puts it near IN (8.68% -> -7.30), but Ohio's situation was materially worse. The Rust Belt manufacturing decline was already underway (5.43% UR, stagnant 0.41% pop growth). Critically, Cleveland, Dayton, and Akron had severe subprime lending concentrations that drove foreclosure waves even without a speculative price bubble. This is a case where the labor market mechanism (job loss -> foreclosure -> price decline) operated independently of bubble dynamics, similar to GA's crash but through a different channel. Expect a worse outcome than Indiana.

### WI (Wisconsin) — predicted: **-15.00**

Appreciation of 22.65% sits between the moderate-crash cluster (NC -13.06, SC -13.02, MO -10.97) and the more severe Midwest group (MN -21.83 at 24.94%, IL -21.81 at 25.87%). Wisconsin shares economic structure with MN and IL (manufacturing, urban metro concentrations), and Milwaukee had meaningful foreclosure issues. However, WI's appreciation is a few points below MN/IL, and Milwaukee's subprime/foreclosure crisis was less extreme than Chicago's or the Twin Cities'. Pop growth (1.80%) is modest, limiting construction oversupply. This lands WI between the two clusters.

### OR (Oregon) — predicted: **-25.00**

The closest comparable is WA (42.91% appreciation -> -25.19). Oregon has slightly more appreciation (44.87%), higher unemployment (5.39% vs 4.99%), and lower income ($34,488 vs $39,744), all suggesting equal or slightly worse vulnerability. Portland's market experienced significant speculative activity, and Bend, OR was one of the most overheated small markets nationally (massive speculative construction). The timber industry was declining. Slightly lower pop growth (3.48% vs 4.37%) partially offsets. Net: nearly identical to Washington.

### VA (Virginia) — predicted: **-24.00**

High appreciation (53.63%) places VA between NJ (48.76% -> -22.13) and MD (65.16% -> -25.30) in the DC corridor. Northern Virginia's exurbs (Prince William, Loudoun counties) saw extreme speculative building and were among the hardest-hit markets nationally. However, VA's very low UR (3.08%) and high PCPI ($42,164) reflect the government/military employment buffer that stabilized DC (-11.93%). The state average blends the devastated NoVA exurbs with more stable markets in Richmond, Hampton Roads, and the Shenandoah Valley. More severe than NJ (which lacked the exurban overbuilding at the same scale), roughly in line with MD adjusted downward for lower appreciation.

### AK (Alaska) — predicted: **-9.00**

Alaska's 36.14% appreciation matches MT (36.61% -> -10.06) numerically, but the economies are quite different. Alaska's high UR (6.62%) is largely structural (seasonal industries, remote communities) and doesn't signal cyclical weakness the same way it would in the lower 48. High PCPI ($41,013) reflects oil wealth. The housing market is small, geographically isolated, and supply-constrained (building in Alaska is expensive). Oil prices crashed in 2008 but recovered relatively quickly, limiting the economic damage. The isolation insulated Alaska from mainland speculative dynamics. Similar to MT but with oil-economy resilience partially offsetting the structural UR.

### WV (West Virginia) — predicted: **-8.00**

Moderate appreciation (25.64%) in isolation would suggest comparisons to MN/IL (-21.8%), but WV is fundamentally different. Very low income ($28,528, lowest in the held-out set), minimal population growth (0.86%), and a coal-dependent rural economy mean there was almost no speculative building or subprime lending at scale. The appreciation likely reflects low base effects and national trends rather than a local bubble. Comparable to rural, low-income states like AR (20.02% -> -6.70) and the lower end of states like MO (18.82% -> -10.97). WV's slightly higher appreciation but very limited speculative dynamics yields a moderate decline.

### CT (Connecticut) — predicted: **-20.00**

Appreciation of 36.58% is in the range of MT (-10.06), ME (-13.11), and PA (-9.38), but Connecticut's economy is structurally distinct: heavy concentration in financial services (Fairfield County hedge funds, Stamford's financial sector) and insurance (Hartford). The 2008 crisis directly gutted these industries. In the Northeast corridor, NJ (-22.13 at 48.76%) and MA (-17.56 at 28.21%) bracket CT's appreciation level. CT's financial sector vulnerability pushes it toward the more severe end. Low pop growth (0.95%) limits construction oversupply, but the income shock from finance-sector layoffs and bonus collapses drove significant price declines, particularly in the Gold Coast. Worse than MA, slightly less than NJ.

### CO (Colorado) — predicted: **-5.00**

Appreciation of 11.88% is identical to NE (11.88% -> -2.88) and similar to TX (11.68% -> -4.15) and KS (12.49% -> -4.97). Colorado experienced an earlier housing correction following the 2000-2003 tech bust and was still recovering, which prevented a major bubble from building. Moderate pop growth (4.23%) reflects genuine economic diversification (tech, defense, outdoor industry) rather than speculation. Moderate UR (4.25%) and high income ($39,869) suggest resilience. Slightly worse than NE due to higher pop growth and some exurban overbuilding east of Denver, but fundamentally not a bubble state in this cycle.

### MI (Michigan) — predicted: **-28.00**

Michigan presents the starkest case of employment-driven crash without a price bubble. Appreciation was only 8.86% (near IN at 8.68% -> -7.30), but the economic context is radically different. The 6.87% UR (highest in the held-out set) was already signaling the auto industry's unraveling before the national crisis. Population was declining (-0.05%). When GM and Chrysler entered bankruptcy in 2008-09, Michigan's unemployment peaked around 14-15%, triggering massive foreclosure waves. The labor market mechanism (job destruction -> mortgage default -> price collapse -> negative equity -> further default) operated at extreme scale. Detroit, Flint, Saginaw, and other auto-dependent cities saw catastrophic declines. The state average is moderated somewhat by relative stability in Ann Arbor, Grand Rapids, and West Michigan, but Michigan's crash was driven by economic collapse rather than bubble popping. This is an amplified version of GA's dynamic (15.17% appreciation -> -24.38% crash from overbuilding); Michigan had less appreciation but far worse employment destruction.

### NH (New Hampshire) — predicted: **-13.50**

Appreciation of 31.83% with very low UR (3.52%) and high income ($42,866). NH's southern tier (Nashua, Manchester, Salem) is part of the Greater Boston commuter belt and saw significant speculative appreciation driven by Boston spillover. Comparable to ME (34.89% -> -13.11) with slightly less appreciation and much better economic fundamentals (lower UR, higher income). MA (-17.56 at 28.21%) is another reference, though MA's crash was driven more by Boston-area subprime issues. VT (-6.55 at 41.92%) is an outlier driven by its uniquely rural/vacation-home market, not applicable here. NH's strong fundamentals (low UR, high income) provide resilience, but the Boston-spillover speculation in southern NH was real. Similar severity to Maine.
