## Analysis Framework

The training data reveals several key drivers of crash severity:

1. **Appreciation (bubble size)** is the dominant predictor, with a convex relationship: crashes accelerate disproportionately above ~60% appreciation.
2. **Population growth amplifies bubbles** by attracting speculative construction. States with both high appreciation AND high pop growth (NV, AZ) crashed hardest.
3. **Structural economic buffers** can moderate crashes. Government employment (DC: 70% appreciation but only -12% crash), island supply constraints (HI: 81% appreciation but only -18%), and resource economies (AK: 36% appreciation, 0% crash) all insulate.
4. **Manufacturing collapse** can cause severe crashes independent of bubbles (MI: 9% appreciation but -29% crash due to auto industry).
5. **Unemployment rate** alone is weakly predictive, but high UR combined with other vulnerabilities amplifies the downturn.

### FL (Florida): -45%

Comparables: AZ (73.44%, pop 9.41% → -46.01%), CA (73.96%, pop 2.18% → -41.04%), NV (79.74%, pop 12.18% → -55.69%). ### MD (Maryland): -25%

Very high appreciation (65.16%) places MD between the moderate-high cluster (NJ at 48.76% → -22.13%, VA at 53.63% → -17.39%) and the extreme cluster (CA/AZ at ~74% → -41 to -46%). The key moderating factor is MD's government employment base (NSA, NIH, federal agencies, defense contractors), similar to what buffers VA and DC. But MD lacks DC's extreme supply constraints, and the outer suburbs of Baltimore and DC exurbs saw significant speculative building. MD's appreciation is ~12 points above VA's; applying a roughly 0.6 point crash penalty per additional appreciation point (discounted for government insulation) pushes MD to about -24 to -26%. Higher income (45,293) and moderate pop growth (2.39%) provide some cushion relative to CA/AZ.

### CT (Connecticut): -19%

Moderate-high appreciation (36.58%) in a wealthy northeastern state. Nearest NE corridor comparables: NH (31.83% → -19.98%), MA (28.21% → -17.56%), NJ (48.76% → -22.13%). CT's appreciation falls between NH and NJ. The very high income (53,224, second only to DC) suggests fewer subprime borrowers and more ability to absorb losses. However, CT's Fairfield County was a hedge fund and trading hub devastated by the financial crisis, and Hartford's insurance industry also suffered. The low pop growth (0.95%) limits overbuilding. Net result: slightly milder than NH despite higher appreciation, due to wealth, but still a meaningful crash driven by financial sector exposure.

### GA (Georgia): -14%

Low appreciation (15.17%) but very high pop growth (6.18%). NC is the closest match (16.94%, pop 5.87% → -13.06%): similar income, UR, and Southern economic profile. Atlanta's exurban sprawl drove speculative construction, and the metro area had significant subprime lending. The high pop growth suggests overbuilding, but the low appreciation caps the downside because home prices didn't have far to fall. TX (11.68%, pop 6.03% → -4.15%) had similar pop growth but was insulated by stricter lending laws and a more diversified economy. GA lacks those protections. Slightly worse than NC due to Atlanta's specific subprime vulnerabilities.

### ME (Maine): -14%

Moderate-high appreciation (34.89%) in a New England state. PA (34.06% → -9.38%) is the closest match by appreciation, but ME's New England location links it to a region that generally crashed harder. NH (31.83% → -19.98%) is the regional comparable. ME has lower income (34,168 vs NH's 42,866) and higher UR (4.71% vs 3.52%), making it more vulnerable to economic shocks. However, low pop growth (1.31%) and ME's largely rural character limit speculative overbuilding. Southern Maine (Portland area) had a genuine bubble, while northern Maine was largely unaffected. The blend produces a moderate crash, worse than PA but milder than NH.

### OH (Ohio): -12%

Very low appreciation (9.43%) means there was almost no bubble to deflate; the crash is driven by economic weakness. The critical comparable is MI (8.86% → -29.40%), but MI's crash was dominated by the auto industry collapse (GM, Chrysler bankruptcies). OH has some auto exposure (Toledo, Cleveland suppliers) but is more diversified: Columbus has government, education, and insurance; Cincinnati has consumer goods. IN (8.68% → -7.30%) is the mild-case Rust Belt comparable. OH's UR (5.43%) sits between IN (5.02%) and MI (6.87%), and its near-zero pop growth (0.41%) signals economic stagnation. Manufacturing losses drove foreclosures and price declines beyond what appreciation alone would predict. Worse than IN, far milder than MI.

### NY (New York): -12%

Moderate-high appreciation (40.29%) but unique market dynamics. NYC's extreme supply constraints and global demand provide a significant floor: Manhattan and Brooklyn rebounded faster than almost anywhere in the country. But NY state also includes Long Island (significant subprime exposure) and upstate (weak manufacturing economy, population decline). Comparables: PA (34.06% → -9.38%), NJ (48.76% → -22.13%). Interpolating between these on appreciation gives roughly -15%, but NYC's insulating effect warrants a 2-3 point discount. The negative pop growth (-0.37%) means minimal speculative overbuilding at the state level.

### MO (Missouri): -10%

Low-moderate appreciation (18.82%) in a heartland state. Closest matches: AL (18.37% → -9.90%), WI (22.65% → -11.81%), TN (17.18% → -8.92%). MO's economic profile (moderate income, moderate UR, low pop growth) places it squarely in this cluster. St. Louis had some post-industrial decline, and Kansas City had moderate suburban growth, but neither metro experienced significant speculative building. The 18.82% appreciation is well below the threshold where bubble dynamics dominate. A straightforward moderate decline driven by general recession effects.

### AR (Arkansas): -8%

Low appreciation (20.02%) in the lowest-income state in the sample (29,376). Comparables: AL (18.37% → -9.90%), MS (16.40% → -8.67%), TN (17.18% → -8.92%), KY (14.25% → -4.45%). AR's slightly higher appreciation (20%) pushes it toward the AL/MS/TN range rather than the KY/OK range. The moderate pop growth (3.56%) suggests some demand-driven construction, but the low income base limited speculative excess. AR's economy (agriculture, retail via Walmart, trucking) was relatively insulated from financial sector dynamics. A mild, recession-driven decline.

### SD (South Dakota): -4%

Low appreciation (17.95%) with the lowest UR in the sample (3.01%). The closest comparable is ND (22.30%, UR 3.12% → 0.00%), but ND's zero crash was partly driven by the Bakken oil boom, which SD doesn't share. ### NE (Nebraska): -2%

Very low appreciation (11.88%) and very low UR (3.04%). The identical appreciation to CO (11.88% → -10.14%) is misleading: CO had higher UR (4.25%), much higher pop growth (4.23%), and Denver's speculative condo market. NE is structurally closer to IA (13.29% → 0.00%) and ND (22.30% → 0.00%): stable, agriculture-anchored Plains economies with tight labor markets and no speculative overbuilding. KS (12.49% → -4.97%) provides an upper bound. NE's fundamentals are sound across the board. Minimal crash.
