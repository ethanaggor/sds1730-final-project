## Analysis and Predictions

### Key Patterns in Training Data

The dominant predictor is **pre-crash appreciation** (bubble size), amplified by **population growth** (proxy for speculative overbuilding). High income and low unemployment provide partial cushions. The "sand states" (NV, FL, CA) show extreme crashes driven by the combination of >70% appreciation and high in-migration. Meanwhile, stable Midwest/Plains states (IA, ND, SD) with <20% appreciation were essentially unscathed.

Two important exceptions reveal secondary drivers:
- **GA** (-24.38% crash with only 15.17% appreciation): Atlanta's subprime exposure and rapid suburban building amplified the decline beyond what appreciation predicted.
- **DC** (-11.93% with 70.23% appreciation): Government employment stability insulated it despite an enormous bubble.

### State-by-State Predictions

**AR (Arkansas)** — Predicted: **-10.5%**
Comps: AL (-9.90%, 18.37% app), TN (-8.92%, 17.18% app), MO (-10.97%, 18.82% app). Arkansas has similar low-moderate appreciation (20.02%) to these southern/border states. Its very low PCPI ($29,376) adds slight vulnerability, and its UR (5.23%) is higher than AL/MO. Not a speculative market; the decline reflects modest pullback in a fundamentals-driven housing economy.

**MN (Minnesota)** — Predicted: **-14.0%**
Comps: WI (-11.81%, 22.65% app), IL (-21.81%, 25.87% app). Minnesota sits between its Midwest neighbors in appreciation (24.94%). Stronger fundamentals than WI (lower UR, higher PCPI) but the Twin Cities had more bubble activity than Milwaukee. Illinois's crash was inflated by severe Chicago-suburban subprime exposure, which MN lacked to the same degree. MN's diversified economy (health care, corporate headquarters, education) provides cushion, landing it several points worse than WI but well below IL.

**NH (New Hampshire)** — Predicted: **-13.0%**
Comps: ME (-13.11%, 34.89% app), PA (-9.38%, 34.06% app), MA (-17.56%, 28.21% app). NH at 31.83% appreciation had a moderate bubble, largely driven by Boston-metro spillover into southern NH. Its fundamentals are strong (3.52% UR, $42,866 PCPI), better than ME and PA, which moderates the decline. However, the dependence on the Greater Boston market, which crashed harder (MA at -17.56%), pulls NH worse than PA. Roughly comparable to ME.

**KS (Kansas)** — Predicted: **-5.0%**
Comps: NE (-2.88%, 11.88% app), OK (-3.57%, 14.28% app), TX (-4.15%, 11.68% app). Kansas at 12.49% appreciation is squarely in the non-bubble Plains tier. Stable, non-speculative market with moderate income and low pop growth. Its UR (4.42%) is higher than NE/OK, adding slight vulnerability, pushing it a couple points worse than Nebraska. No meaningful speculative activity.

**CT (Connecticut)** — Predicted: **-18.0%**
Comps: NJ (-22.13%, 48.76% app), NY (-14.02%, 40.29% app), MA (-17.56%, 28.21% app). CT's 36.58% appreciation is below NJ and NY, but its economy is heavily dependent on financial services (Stamford hedge funds, Hartford insurance). The 2008 crisis directly gutted CT's employment base in a way not captured by the 2006 features. Its very high PCPI ($53,224) provides some buffer (as with DC), but unlike DC, CT lacks guaranteed government employment. CT's prolonged, slow recovery is consistent with a deeper trough than NY, placing it near MA's level despite higher appreciation.

**AZ (Arizona)** — Predicted: **-49.0%**
Comps: NV (-55.69%, 79.74% app, 12.18% pop), FL (-44.73%, 74.37% app, 6.84% pop), CA (-41.04%, 73.96% app, 2.18% pop). Arizona is a quintessential sand-state bubble. Its appreciation (73.44%) matches CA and FL, and its population growth (9.41%) is between FL and NV, indicating massive speculative overbuilding (Phoenix exurbs, Maricopa County). Its PCPI ($34,460) is the lowest of the sand states, implying greater subprime exposure. Interpolating between FL and NV based on population growth yields approximately -50%. AZ was an epicenter of speculative flipping, no-doc lending, and desert subdivision overbuilding.

**WY (Wyoming)** — Predicted: **-9.0%**
Comps: MT (-10.06%, 36.61% app), CO (-10.14%, 11.88% app). Wyoming's profile (34.89% appreciation, 3.07% UR, $43,805 PCPI) is closest to Montana but with significantly higher income and lower UR. Wyoming's energy-dependent economy (oil, gas, coal) provided employment stability through much of the downturn. The state's small, remote housing market saw limited speculative capital inflow. Some resort-market vulnerability (Jackson Hole), but moderated at the state level. Better fundamentals than MT warrant a slightly smaller decline.

**OR (Oregon)** — Predicted: **-25.0%**
Comps: WA (-25.19%, 42.91% app), ID (-26.95%, 39.45% app), DE (-20.44%, 45.63% app). Oregon (44.87% appreciation) is the natural Pacific Northwest pair with Washington. OR has slightly higher appreciation, higher UR (5.39% vs 4.99%), and notably lower income ($34,488 vs $39,744). Portland had significant bubble activity, and Oregon's economy is less diversified than Washington's (which had Microsoft, Amazon, Boeing). The weaker fundamentals roughly offset the similar appreciation, landing OR very close to WA's decline.

**AK (Alaska)** — Predicted: **-11.0%**
Comps: MT (-10.06%, 36.61% app), ME (-13.11%, 34.89% app). Alaska is unique: remote, oil-dependent, and structurally insulated from national speculative dynamics. Its 36.14% appreciation is moderate, but its 6.62% UR reflects structural features of the Alaska labor market (seasonal work, remote communities), not pre-crisis cyclical weakness in the same way as Michigan. Oil revenues, military base employment, and geographic isolation limited speculative capital inflow. The high PCPI ($41,013) provides cushion. A moderate decline driven partially by the 2008 oil price collapse, but buffered by the market's isolation.

**MI (Michigan)** — Predicted: **-18.0%**
Comps: IN (-7.30%, 8.68% app), GA (-24.38%, 15.17% app). Michigan is the critical outlier case: minimal appreciation (8.86%) but the worst pre-crash UR in the dataset (6.87%) and negative population growth. The auto industry was already in crisis before 2008; the financial collapse accelerated layoffs, foreclosures, and demand destruction. This is a fundamentals-driven crash, not a bubble-driven one. Detroit's collapse (where prices fell 50%+) dominates the state figure, moderated by more stable metros (Ann Arbor, Grand Rapids). Much worse than IN (which had lower UR, positive pop growth) despite similar appreciation. GA demonstrates that non-appreciation factors can drive severe crashes, and MI's economic distress was more acute.

**OH (Ohio)** — Predicted: **-11.5%**
Comps: IN (-7.30%, 8.68% app), WV (-7.26%, 25.64% app), MI (predicted -18.0%). Ohio (9.43% appreciation, 5.43% UR) is a less extreme version of Michigan: Rust Belt manufacturing decline, elevated unemployment, near-zero population growth, and early subprime exposure (Cleveland was a national foreclosure epicenter pre-2008). But Ohio's economy is more diversified than Michigan's (Columbus government/education sector, Cincinnati corporate headquarters), and its UR is a full 1.4 points lower. Ohio's crash exceeds Indiana's due to worse labor market conditions and population stagnation, but falls well short of Michigan's auto-driven collapse.
