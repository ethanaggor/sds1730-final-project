## Analysis and Predictions

### Framework

The training data reveals several key drivers of crash severity:

1. **Appreciation (bubble size)** is the primary driver, but not sufficient alone (DC: 70% appreciation, only -12% crash)
2. **Population growth** amplifies crashes through overbuilding and speculative demand (NV: 12.18% growth, -55.69% crash)
3. **Economic structure** can buffer (DC's government jobs, HI's supply constraints) or amplify (GA's overbuilding despite only 15% appreciation drove a -24% crash)
4. **Income/unemployment** are secondary modifiers

Notable training data patterns:
- Stable non-speculative economies (IA, ND, SD) resisted entirely (0% crash)
- The NV outlier (-55.69) shows that extreme appreciation + extreme pop growth is catastrophic
- GA (-24.38 on only 15.17% appreciation) proves that overbuilding and subprime exposure can drive severe crashes independent of bubble size

### State-by-State Predictions

**FL (appreciation: 74.37, pop_growth: 6.84)**

Florida was an epicenter of speculation: condos flipped multiple times in Miami, massive suburban sprawl in Tampa and Orlando, tourism-dependent economy that would contract sharply. Appreciation (74.37%) is near NV levels (79.74%), but pop growth (6.84%) is roughly half of NV's (12.18%), limiting the overbuilding surplus. No natural supply constraints (unlike HI) or economic anchor (unlike DC). Comparables: NV (-55.69, more extreme in both dimensions), HI (-18.48, island constraints). FL fills the gap, closer to NV given the absence of stabilizing factors.

**Prediction: -44.00**

---

**AZ (appreciation: 73.44, pop_growth: 9.41)**

Phoenix was the suburban sprawl and flipping capital. AZ's pop growth (9.41%) is significantly higher than FL's (6.84%) and approaches NV territory (12.18%), meaning even more overbuilding. Lower income (34,460) than FL (38,009) means less household resilience. The economy was heavily construction and real-estate dependent, a feedback loop that accelerates decline. AZ should be worse than FL and the closest analog to NV among the held-out states.

**Prediction: -48.00**

---

**CA (appreciation: 73.96, pop_growth: 2.18)**

CA had the same extreme appreciation as FL and AZ but much lower pop growth (2.18%). Coastal areas (SF, LA) had zoning-driven supply constraints that limited overbuilding. However, inland markets (Riverside, Stockton, Bakersfield, Sacramento) had massive overbuilding and severe subprime exposure. The statewide crash is a population-weighted mix of resilient coast and devastated interior. Higher income (41,454) provides some buffer. Training comparables at this appreciation level (DC at -11.93, HI at -18.48) both had special stability factors CA lacks. Scaling from normal high-appreciation states (NJ: 48.76% appreciation, -22.13 crash) suggests CA, with 50% more appreciation, should be substantially worse than NJ.

**Prediction: -40.00**

---

**MD (appreciation: 65.16, pop_growth: 2.39, pcpi: 45,293)**

Maryland's housing market is split: DC-adjacent suburbs (Montgomery County) benefit from government employment stability, while Baltimore and outer suburbs (Prince George's County) had significant subprime exposure and overbuilding. Appreciation (65.16%) is between VA (53.63%, crash -17.39) and DC (70.23%, crash -11.93). MD lacks DC's pure government-job base but gets spillover. Higher appreciation than VA should drive a worse crash. NJ (-22.13 at 48.76% appreciation) and DE (-20.44 at 45.63%) provide lower-appreciation anchors; MD's much higher appreciation suggests it should be worse. Partially offset by high income (45,293) and moderate pop growth.

**Prediction: -24.00**

---

**WA (appreciation: 42.91, pop_growth: 4.37)**

Washington (Seattle metro primarily) had appreciation similar to OR (44.87%, crash -25.98) and pop growth slightly higher (4.37% vs 3.48%). However, WA's tech economy (Microsoft, Amazon, Boeing) provides substantially more resilience than OR's. Also comparable to ID (39.45% appreciation, 7.72% pop growth, -26.95 crash), which had more pop growth but less economic resilience. WA should be moderately better than OR.

**Prediction: -22.00**

---

**NH (appreciation: 31.83, pop_growth: 2.23, pcpi: 42,866)**

New England state with moderate appreciation, bracketed by MA (28.21%, -17.56) and ME (34.89%, -13.11). NH has the highest income of the three (42,866) and lowest unemployment (3.52), suggesting more resilience. But southern NH (Nashua, Manchester) was part of the Boston bubble spillover. Pop growth (2.23%) higher than both MA and ME, adding modest overbuilding risk. NH's appreciation falls between MA and ME, and its strong economic indicators place it in the middle of their crash range.

**Prediction: -15.00**

---

**MI (appreciation: 8.86, ur: 6.87, pop_growth: -0.05)**

Michigan is a unique case: virtually no bubble (8.86% appreciation, lowest in the dataset) but the most severe pre-existing economic decline. The 2006 unemployment rate of 6.87 was already the highest in the data, and auto industry collapse would push it far higher. Negative pop growth signals structural decline. In the training data, low-appreciation states typically crash -3 to -10%. But GA shows that non-bubble factors can add 14+ points of crash severity (15.17% appreciation, -24.38 crash). MI's economic collapse through foreclosures and demand destruction, rather than bubble deflation, is the primary driver. No overbuilding (unlike GA), but the foreclosure cascade and Detroit's long-term decline add significant downside.

**Prediction: -20.00**

---

**OH (appreciation: 9.43, ur: 5.43, pop_growth: 0.41)**

Rust Belt state with very low appreciation and near-zero pop growth. Similar economic profile to MI but less severe: lower unemployment (5.43 vs 6.87), positive (barely) pop growth, and a more diversified economy (Columbus services sector alongside Cleveland/Cincinnati manufacturing). Subprime exposure in Cleveland adds downside beyond what appreciation alone would suggest. In the training data, comparable low-appreciation states with economic headwinds (CO at 11.88%, -10.14; MO at 18.82%, -10.97) provide a baseline around -10 to -11. OH's Rust Belt vulnerability adds a few points.

**Prediction: -13.00**

---

**AK (appreciation: 36.14, ur: 6.62, pop_growth: 4.15)**

Alaska is isolated and small-market. Appreciation (36.14%) and pop growth (4.15%) are very similar to MT (36.61%, 3.60%, crash -10.06). AK's high unemployment (6.62) is structural (seasonal oil/fishing employment) rather than a sign of bubble vulnerability. The oil economy provided income stability through much of the crisis, and geographic isolation limited speculative activity. AK should be close to MT with some downward pressure from higher unemployment.

**Prediction: -11.00**

---

**WY (appreciation: 34.89, ur: 3.07, pcpi: 43,805)**

Very small, energy-dependent market. Nearly identical appreciation to MT (36.61%, -10.06) with even better fundamentals: lower unemployment (3.07 vs 3.37), much higher income (43,805 vs 31,870). Energy sector (coal, natural gas) was strong through much of the period. PA (34.06% appreciation, -9.38) is another comparable. WY's economic strength suggests it should perform slightly better than MT.

**Prediction: -9.00**

---

**IN (appreciation: 8.68, ur: 5.02, pop_growth: 2.20)**

The lowest appreciation in the held-out set. Very little bubble to deflate. Comparables in the training data at this appreciation level (TX: 11.68%, -4.15; NE: 11.88%, -2.88; KS: 12.49%, -4.97) show crashes of -3 to -5%. IN has auto industry exposure that adds vulnerability beyond these comparables, but its economy is more diversified than MI's (Indianapolis services, Subaru/Toyota manufacturing spread across the state). Moderate unemployment (5.02) and pop growth (2.20) are unremarkable. The crash should be mild but somewhat worse than the -3 to -5% baseline due to manufacturing sensitivity.

**Prediction: -8.00**
